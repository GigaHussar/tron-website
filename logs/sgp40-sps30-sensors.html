More Air Quality Sensors: SGP40 and the SPS30 Problem

March 2026

#hardware#smart_home#automation

After the CO2 sensor was running reliably, I wanted more data. Two more sensors made the list: an SGP40 for VOCs — volatile organic compounds — and an SPS30 for particulate matter. More things to measure. More things to worry about.

The SGP40 went in without a fight. Wired it up, added it to ESPHome, and it was reporting readings within minutes. Clean setup, no surprises.

The SPS30 was different.

I ordered it, prepared everything — cables, config, the usual routine. Then I actually looked at the datasheet. The SPS30 needs 5V. Almost every small sensor I'd worked with runs on 3.3V, the native voltage of the ESP32. Not this one.

The board I had from AliExpress had a pin labeled 5V, so I figured that was it. Wired everything up, powered it on. Nothing. The sensor wasn't responding.

I started researching. After digging through documentation and dead ends, I found the answer on an electronics forum. Someone had posted about the exact board I was using — apparently, by default, that 5V pin is configured to receive power rather than supply it. To switch it into output mode, you need to bridge two small pads on the board with solder.

The pads were tiny. My soldering iron was not. But I decided to try anyway.

It wasn't easy. The board is small enough that it felt like surgery with a shovel. I managed to get the solder bridge in without destroying anything nearby — barely.

Powered everything on again. Still nothing.

At this point I was genuinely frustrated. I'd already troubleshot the hardware, done the soldering, double-checked the config. Then I went back to the physical connections and looked at every cable one by one. And there it was: two cables swapped. A simple wiring mistake. The kind that makes you feel both relieved and slightly embarrassed.

Swapped them. Everything came to life immediately.
