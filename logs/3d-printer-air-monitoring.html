Monitoring the Air Inside My 3D Printer

April 2026

#hardware#smart_home#automation#3d_printing

Once the SGP40 and SPS30 were running in my room, a natural next question came up: what's the air like inside the printer itself? Filament heating up, fans running, plastic melting — it seemed worth knowing. And a secondary idea crept in alongside it: what if something went seriously wrong? Overheating plastic, a clog, or worse — a fire. A 3D printer running unattended in a garage feels like the right place for a smoke detector.

Finding the right detector took longer than expected. Most of what's available is either battery-powered or cloud-dependent — neither of which I wanted. Batteries degrade quickly above 40–50°C, and temperatures inside a running printer regularly exceed that. Cloud dependency I avoid on principle. Eventually I found one on AliExpress that fit: a plain smoke detector running on 12V, with two signal cables coming out. No app, no subscription, no batteries. Exactly what I needed.

Setting it up was a different story. The instructions from AliExpress were difficult to follow — sometimes they referenced "12C" where they clearly meant I2C, likely a translation issue, but it introduced just enough uncertainty to slow everything down. I spent most of the day second-guessing connections I should have been confident about.

After a lot of doubts and backtracking, I got it working. Or at least — it's connected, it's reporting, and Home Assistant sees it. Whether it actually detects smoke is a question I haven't answered yet. Testing a smoke detector properly requires smoke, and I haven't set up a controlled way to do that without just hoping it triggers. That's still pending.

The longer-term plan is to also mount the SPS30 and SGP40 inside the printer enclosure. More sensors, more data — particulate matter and VOCs from heated filament are worth tracking in their own right, not just as a safety measure. Knowing what's happening inside the printer is better than not knowing.
