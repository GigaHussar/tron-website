Going Local: My Home Assistant Setup

January 2026

#hardware#network#smart_home

At the start of the year I started hearing more about smart home setups. Most of it went in one ear and out the other — until I read about Google Nest losing support for older devices. That one stuck.

I don't like the idea of a big tech company deciding when my hardware stops working. You buy a device, set it up, it becomes part of your home — and then someone flips a switch in a data center and it's a paperweight. That's not how I want to run things. So I started looking for something local, something I actually control.

That's how I found Home Assistant. Open source, runs on your own hardware, no cloud required. The more I read, the more it made sense.

I had an old Lenovo laptop with a touchscreen sitting around — the Yoga 500. My original vision was to turn it into a kind of permanent tablet: mount it somewhere, host Home Assistant on it, and control everything right from the screen. It sounded elegant. A dedicated panel, always on, always local.

I installed Ubuntu on the laptop. The plan was to run HAOS — the official Home Assistant Operating System — inside a KVM virtual machine, with Ubuntu as the host. Ubuntu recognized the touchscreen immediately, no extra setup needed. The idea was that Ubuntu would open a browser pointed at the Home Assistant IP, and the touchscreen would handle the rest.

The first real fight was networking. I wanted the VM to connect through Wi-Fi — seemed simple enough. It wasn't. Bridging a wireless interface with KVM is genuinely painful. After a long battle I gave up and ran an Ethernet cable instead. Suddenly everything worked. Sometimes the boring solution is the right one.

One thing I did put real thought into was the battery. A laptop running 24/7 plugged in will degrade its battery fast. I configured the system to only charge the battery up to around 60%, then run purely off the power supply. The battery only charges again when it drops significantly. That way the battery stays healthy longer, and it acts as a small UPS buffer against brief blackouts.

After that, setting up HAOS itself was straightforward. I installed the Home Assistant app on my phone and connected it to the local instance. It worked.

In practice, I ended up using the phone app for everything. The touchscreen panel idea is still sitting in the back of my head — maybe someday, maybe a different location in the apartment. But knowing it's possible is enough for now. The important part is that everything runs locally, on hardware I own, with no subscription and no expiry date.
